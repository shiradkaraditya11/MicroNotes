Module 01
Foundations of Economics
1.1 Opportunity Cost & Sunk Cost
Opportunity Cost

The value of the next-best alternative you give up when making a choice. Every decision has an opportunity cost — it's the true economic cost of any action.

Opportunity Cost
OC = Value of the best foregone alternative
Not just money — includes time, effort, and any foregone benefit.

Example — Ramesh the Plumber

Ramesh earns ₹500/day. He takes 2 days off to study. Workshop fee = ₹0. But economic cost = ₹1,000 (2 days' foregone wages). The accountant sees ₹0; the economist sees ₹1,000.

Sunk Cost

A cost that has already been paid and cannot be recovered, regardless of future decisions. It's gone — treat it as irrelevant.

⚠️
MCQ Trap — Sunk Cost Fallacy: "We already spent ₹5 lakh, so we must continue." This is wrong. Sunk costs are irrelevant to forward-looking decisions. Never factor them in.
Cost TypeRecoverable?Relevant to Decisions?Example
Opportunity CostN/A✅ AlwaysForegone salary
Sunk Cost❌ Never❌ NeverNon-refundable ticket
Fixed CostOnly if you exit✅ Affects profitRent
1.2 The Marginal Principle
Core Principle

Increase an activity if Marginal Benefit (MB) ≥ Marginal Cost (MC). The optimal point is where MB = MC. Economists always think at the margin — one more unit.

Optimal Decision Rule
Do more if: MB ≥ MC
Stop when: MB = MC ← optimal point

Example: Study one more hour if the exam score gain (MB) ≥ cost of your time + fatigue (MC). Stop adding study hours once MB = MC.

The Marginal Principle — Optimal Point at MB = MC

1.4 Law of Demand
Law of Demand

As price increases, quantity demanded decreases — an inverse relationship — ceteris paribus (all else equal). Demand curves slope downward.

Linear Demand Function
Qd = a − b·P
a = autonomous demand (intercept). b = responsiveness to price (always positive coefficient, but negative relationship). Inverse form: P = (a/b) − (1/b)·Q
Full Demand Function
Qx = a − b·Px + c·Py − d·I + e·Ax + ...
Px = own price, Py = related good price, I = income, Ax = advertising. Signs depend on whether goods are substitutes/complements/normal/inferior.

Why Demand Slopes Downward — 3 Reasons

  • Substitution Effect: Higher price → switch to cheaper alternatives.
  • Income Effect: Higher price → real purchasing power falls → buy less.
  • Diminishing Marginal Utility: Each extra unit gives less satisfaction → willing to pay less for it.
Demand Curve — Downward Sloping
1.5 Law of Supply
Law of Supply

As price increases, quantity supplied increases — a positive relationship — ceteris paribus. Higher prices make production more profitable, attracting more output.

Linear Supply Function
Qs = c + d·P
d is always positive (upward sloping). Example from course: Qs = 2000 + 3Px − 4Py − Pi

2.1 Market Equilibrium
Market Equilibrium

The price (P*) and quantity (Q*) where Qd = Qs. The market clears — no surplus, no shortage.

How to Find Equilibrium
Step 1: Set Qd = Qs
Step 2: Solve for P*
Step 3: Substitute P* back to find Q*
SituationMeaningMarket Response
Price above P*Surplus (excess supply)Sellers lower price → return to P*
Price below P*Shortage (excess demand)Buyers bid price up → return to P*

2.2 Invisible Hand, Consumer & Producer Surplus
Invisible Hand (Adam Smith)

Individual self-interest in competitive markets leads to socially efficient outcomes — as if guided by an "invisible hand." No central planner needed; the market self-organises.

Consumer Surplus (CS)

What consumers were willing to pay minus what they actually paid.
= Area below demand curve, above price line (P*).

Producer Surplus (PS)

What producers received minus their minimum acceptable price.
= Area above supply curve, below price line (P*).

Total Surplus (Social Welfare)
Total Surplus = CS + PS
Maximised at competitive equilibrium (P*, Q*). Any artificial distortion (price control, tax, monopoly) creates Deadweight Loss — a reduction in total surplus.
Consumer Surplus & Producer Surplus
✅
At competitive equilibrium, Total Surplus is maximised. Any deviation (price floor, price ceiling, monopoly) creates deadweight loss — value destroyed that benefits no one.
2.3 Price Elasticity of Demand
Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

Measures how sensitive quantity demanded is to a price change. If price rises 1%, by what % does Qd change?

Price Elasticity of Demand
εp = (% ΔQd) / (% ΔP) = (ΔQ/Q) / (ΔP/P) = (ΔQ/ΔP) × (P/Q)
Always negative (demand slopes down). Compare using absolute value |εp|.
Midpoint Formula (more accurate between two points)
εp = [(Q₂−Q₁) / ((Q₂+Q₁)/2)] ÷ [(P₂−P₁) / ((P₂+P₁)/2)]
|εp|TypeMeaningExample
> 1ElasticQd changes more than PLuxury goods, branded items
< 1InelasticQd changes less than PInsulin, salt, petrol
= 1Unit ElasticQd changes same % as PMidpoint of linear demand
= 0Perfectly InelasticQd doesn't change at allLife-saving medication
= ∞Perfectly ElasticAny price rise → Qd = 0Perfectly competitive firm

Determinants of Elasticity

  • Availability of substitutes: More substitutes = more elastic (Pepsi vs. Coke)
  • Necessity vs. luxury: Necessities are inelastic; luxuries are elastic
  • Time horizon: More elastic in long run (more time to adapt)
  • Budget share: Larger share of income = more elastic
  • Narrow definition: "Food" is inelastic; "Dominos pizza" is elastic
📌
Key fact for linear demand: The top half of the curve is elastic (|ε|>1), midpoint is unit elastic, bottom half is inelastic. Elasticity changes along the line even though the slope is constant.
2.4 Income & Cross-Price Elasticity
Income Elasticity of Demand (YED)
εM = (% ΔQd) / (% ΔIncome) = (ΔQ/Q) / (ΔM/M)
Sign tells you if good is normal (+) or inferior (−). Magnitude tells you if it's a luxury (>1) or necessity (0 to 1).
εMGood TypeBehaviour
> 1Luxury / SuperiorDemand rises faster than income (designer goods)
0 to 1Normal / NecessityDemand rises with income, but slower (food, clothing)
< 0Inferior GoodDemand falls when income rises (instant noodles, cheap bread)
Cross-Price Elasticity (XED)
εXY = (% ΔQx) / (% ΔPy) = (ΔQx/Qx) / (ΔPy/Py)
Measures how demand for good X responds to a price change in good Y.
εXYRelationshipLogicExample
> 0SubstitutesPy↑ → switch to X → Qx↑Tea & Coffee

2.6 Tax Incidence & Deadweight Loss
Tax Incidence

Who actually bears the burden of a tax — buyers, sellers, or both. This is determined by relative elasticity, NOT by who legally pays the tax.

⚠️
Golden Rule: The more inelastic side bears more of the tax burden. If demand is inelastic (like petrol), consumers bear most of it. If supply is inelastic, producers bear most.
ScenarioWho Bears More?
Demand inelastic, Supply elasticConsumers bear more
Demand elastic, Supply inelasticProducers bear more
Perfectly inelastic demandConsumers bear 100%
Perfectly inelastic supplyProducers bear 100%
Tax Revenue & Deadweight Loss
Tax Revenue = t × Q_after_tax
DWL (tax) = ½ × t × ΔQ
t = per-unit tax. ΔQ = reduction in quantity. DWL = value destroyed by the tax that goes to no one.

A tax creates a wedge between buyer price (Pb) and seller price (Ps): Pb − Ps = t. Equilibrium quantity falls below Q*, destroying some total surplus.

Module 03
Behavior of Firms & Cost Theory
3.1 Economic vs. Accounting Profit
Accounting Profit
  • TR minus explicit costs only
  • Explicit = actual cash paid out
  • What your accountant reports
  • Ignores opportunity cost of owner's inputs
Economic Profit
  • TR minus explicit + implicit costs
  • Implicit = opportunity costs (foregone salary, return on capital)
  • What economists use for decisions
  • Always ≤ accounting profit
Profit Formulas
Accounting Profit = TR − Explicit Costs
Economic Profit = TR − Explicit Costs − Implicit Costs
Economic Profit = Accounting Profit − Implicit Costs
Zero economic profit = "normal profit." The firm earns exactly its opportunity cost. This is the long-run competitive equilibrium.
📌
Zero economic profit ≠ zero accounting profit. Zero economic profit means the firm covers all costs including opportunity costs — no incentive to enter or exit.
3.2 Production Function & Marginal Product
Production Function

Shows the maximum output (q) achievable from combinations of inputs — Labour (L) and Capital (K). Short run: K fixed. Long run: both variable.

Production Function & Marginal Product
q = f(L, K)
MPL = ΔQ / ΔL (extra output from one more worker)
Diminishing Marginal Product

As you add more of one input (e.g., workers) while holding others fixed, each extra unit adds progressively less to output. The 10th worker adds less than the 1st — they compete for the same machines.

This is why MC curves eventually slope upward — each additional unit of output requires more labour (at higher cost) as MPL declines.

Returns to Scale (Long Run Only)

TypeWhat HappensLRATC Shape
Increasing Returns to ScaleDouble inputs → more than double outputFalling (economies of scale)
Constant Returns to ScaleDouble inputs → exactly double outputFlat
Decreasing Returns to ScaleDouble inputs → less than double outputRising (diseconomies of scale)
3.3 Cost Categories — Fixed, Variable, Sunk
TypeChanges with Q?Recoverable?Affects Exit?Example
Variable Cost (VC)✅ Yes✅ (stop producing)YesRaw materials, hourly wages
Fixed Cost (FC)❌ NoOnly if you exitYesRent, machinery lease
Sunk Cost❌ No❌ NeverNoR&D already spent, non-refundable licence
⚠️
Fixed vs. Sunk — Critical Difference: Fixed costs affect your profit and exit decision (leave the market and you save FC). Sunk costs are gone regardless — even if you exit, sunk costs cannot be recovered.
Total Cost
TC = FC + VC
Profit (π) = TR − TC = TR − FC − VC
Short Run
  • At least one input is fixed
  • FC > 0
  • Capital (K) is fixed
  • Only labour adjusts
Long Run
  • All inputs are variable
  • FC = 0
  • Both K and L adjust
  • New firms can enter/exit
3.4 All Cost Curves — AFC, AVC, ATC, MC
Per-Unit (Average) Cost Formulas
AFC = FC / Q (Fixed cost per unit)
AVC = VC / Q (Variable cost per unit)
ATC = TC / Q = AFC + AVC
MC = ΔTC / ΔQ = ΔVC / ΔQ
MC uses only VC in the formula (fixed cost doesn't change, so it doesn't affect marginal cost).
CurveShapeReason
AFCAlways falling (hyperbola)Same fixed cost spread over more and more units
AVCU-shapedFalls initially (efficiency), then rises (diminishing returns)
ATCU-shapedAFC always pulls down; AVC eventually dominates upward
MCU-shaped (steeper)Falls at first, then rises steeply due to diminishing MPL
⚡
Must-know MC–ATC relationship:
MC < ATC → ATC is falling
MC > ATC → ATC is rising
MC = ATC → ATC is at its minimum (Efficient Scale)
Same logic applies between MC and AVC.
Cost Curves — MC intersects AVC & ATC at their minimums
3.5 Revenue, Profit Max & Shutdown Decisions
Revenue — Perfect Competition
TR = P × Q | AR = P | MR = P (constant)
In perfect competition, the firm is a price taker. The demand curve facing the firm is perfectly horizontal.
Profit Maximisation Rule
Produce where: MR = MC (i.e. P = MC in perfect competition)
⚠️
Shutdown vs. Exit — Exam Favourite:
Short-run shutdown: Stop producing if P < min AVC (can't cover variable costs).
Long-run exit: Leave market if P < min ATC (can't cover all costs).
Continue even at a loss if min AVC ≤ P < min ATC — losses are smaller than shutting down (you cover VC and some FC).
Price ConditionShort-Run DecisionLong-Run Decision
P > ATCProduce — profit ✓Produce (new firms enter)
AVC ≤ P < ATCProduce (loss, but covers VC)Exit market
P < AVCShut down — stop producingExit immediately
Short-Run Supply Curve

The firm's MC curve above the minimum AVC point. Below min AVC, the firm supplies Q = 0 (shutdown).

Long-Run Supply Curve

The firm's MC curve above the minimum ATC point. Below min ATC, the firm exits permanently.

3.6 Long-Run Competitive Equilibrium
Long-Run Equilibrium

Profits attract new entrants; losses cause exits. This continues until economic profit = 0. At that point: P = MC = min ATC.

Long-Run Equilibrium Conditions
P* = MR = MC = min ATC → Economic Profit = 0

Worked Problem (From Course)

Given: TC = 50 + ½Q² · Market Demand: Q = 120 − P · 9 identical firms. Find SR equilibrium, profit, and LR equilibrium.

Firm costs: FC = 50, VC = ½Q², MC = Q, AVC = Q/2, ATC = 50/Q + Q/2

Individual supply: Set P = MC → P = Q → Individual supply: Q = P

Market supply (9 firms): Qs = 9P

SR Equilibrium: 120 − P = 9P → P* = 12, Q* = 108. Each firm produces Q = 12.

SR Profit: TR = 144, TC = 50 + 72 = 122. Profit = $22 > 0 → Firms enter!

LR Equilibrium: Min ATC: set MC = ATC → Q = 50/Q + Q/2 → Q² = 100 → Q = 10. Min ATC = MC = $10.

LR outcome: P* = 10, Q* = 110 (from demand). Each firm produces 10. Number of firms = 110/10 = 11. Zero economic profit. ✓

Module 04
Monopoly & Market Power
4.1 What is a Monopoly?
Monopoly

A market with a single seller who produces a good with no close substitutes. The monopolist is a price maker — it controls price by choosing how much to produce.

Three Causes of Monopoly

CauseMechanismExample
Government regulationPatents, copyrights, licences grant exclusive rightsPharmaceutical patents, music copyright
Control of scarce resourceOwnership of a unique input blocks competitionDe Beers diamonds (historically)
Natural monopolyATC falls continuously — one firm is cheaper than twoElectricity grid, water pipes, railways
Natural Monopoly

Occurs when a firm has very high fixed costs and near-zero marginal costs — ATC keeps falling as output increases. The whole market is cheapest to serve with one firm. Breaking it up would waste resources.

Monopoly vs. Perfect Competition

FeaturePerfect CompetitionMonopoly
Number of sellersManyOne
Price controlPrice taker (P given)Price maker (chooses P via Q)
Demand curveHorizontal (perfectly elastic)Downward sloping = market demand
MR vs. PriceMR = PMR < P always
Long-run profitZero (π = 0)Positive (persistent economic profit)
EfficiencyP = MC (allocatively efficient)P > MC (allocatively inefficient)
4.2 Monopolist's Demand & Profit Maximisation

The monopolist faces the entire market demand curve (downward sloping). To sell more, it must lower the price — not just for the extra unit, but for all units.

💡
Why MR < P: Selling one more unit earns P on that unit, but requires lowering the price on all previous units sold. The revenue lost on existing units makes MR always less than P. MR lies below the demand curve.
Monopolist's MR Formula (Linear Demand)
If Demand: P = A − BQ → MR = A − 2BQ
MR has the SAME intercept as demand but TWICE the slope. Always below the demand curve.
Monopolist Profit Maximisation — 3 Steps
Step 1: Set MR = MC → find Qm (monopoly quantity)
Step 2: Find Pm from the demand curve at Qm
Step 3: Profit = (Pm − ATC) × Qm
Result: Pm > competitive price. Qm < competitive quantity.
Monopoly — MR = MC gives Qm; Pm read off Demand Curve; DWL = shaded triangle

SpaceX Worked Example

Given: Demand: Q = 8 − P (→ P = 8 − Q) · TC = 3 + 2Q → MC = 2. Find Qm, Pm, profit, and DWL.

MR: P = 8 − Q → MR = 8 − 2Q

Set MR = MC: 8 − 2Q = 2 → Qm = 3

Pm from demand: P = 8 − 3 = $5

Profit: TR = 15, TC = 9. Profit = $6

Competitive outcome: P = MC → P = 2, Qc = 6

DWL: ½ × (5 − 2) × (6 − 3) = $4.50

4.3 Social Cost of Monopoly — Deadweight Loss
Deadweight Loss Formula
DWL = ½ × (Pm − MC) × (Qc − Qm)
The triangle between Qm and Qc. Transactions that would mutually benefit buyers and sellers that never happen because of monopoly.
SurplusCompetitive MarketMonopoly
Consumer SurplusLargeReduced (area B+C transferred/lost)
Producer SurplusSmallerLarger (gains area B from consumers)
Deadweight LossZeroTriangle C+E — gone forever
Total SurplusMaximumBelow maximum
💡
Why monopoly is inefficient: Monopolist sets P > MC. Consumers who value the good above MC but below Pm don't buy — even though the sale would benefit both parties. This is the deadweight loss.
4.4 Price Discrimination — All Three Degrees
Price Discrimination

Charging different prices to different consumers for the same product, based on willingness to pay — to capture more consumer surplus as profit.

📋
Two conditions required: (1) Market power — must be able to set prices. (2) No arbitrage — buyers who get cheap prices can't resell to others at high prices.

1st Degree — Perfect Price Discrimination

1st Degree

Charge each consumer their exact reservation price (maximum they'd pay). Extracts 100% of consumer surplus.

  • No deadweight loss — produces the competitive quantity
  • All consumer surplus goes to the producer
  • MR = AR = P = MC at the margin
  • Practically impossible (requires perfect information about each buyer)
  • Examples: personalised negotiation, some auction models

2nd Degree — Quantity-Based Pricing

2nd Degree

Different prices for different quantity blocks. Buyers self-select based on how much they purchase.

  • Also called block pricing or non-linear pricing
  • Firm doesn't need to know individual willingness to pay
  • Examples: electricity (first 100 units at rate A, next 200 at rate B), telecom data plans, bulk discounts

3rd Degree — Group-Based Pricing

3rd Degree

Different prices charged to different identifiable groups based on observable characteristics correlated with willingness to pay.

3rd Degree Equilibrium
MR₁ = MR₂ = MC
Set MR equal across all market segments and equal to MC. The less elastic group pays the higher price.
📌
Price–Elasticity Rule: Higher price is charged to the less elastic group. Students pay less (elastic, many substitutes). Business travellers pay more (inelastic, must travel). Formula: MR = P(1 + 1/ε) → equalise MR across groups → higher |ε| → lower P.
TypeBased OnCS CapturedDWLReal Example
1st DegreeIndividual willingness100%ZeroPersonalised negotiation
2nd DegreeQuantity purchasedPartialReducedUtility block pricing
3rd DegreeObservable groupPartialMay remainStudent/senior discounts, airline classes

MyArt Worked Example

Given: Professionals: 50 people, value = $700. Amateurs: 50 people, value = $220. MC = $20.

Uniform price $700 (sell only to professionals): Profit = (700−20)×50 = $34,000

Uniform price $220 (sell to everyone): Profit = (220−20)×100 = $20,000 (worse!)

3rd Degree PD ($700 professionals, $220 amateurs):
Profit = (700−20)×50 + (220−20)×50 = $34,000 + $10,000 = $44,000 ← Best outcome

Price discrimination increased profit by $10,000 over the best uniform price strategy.

4.5 Lerner Index — Measuring Market Power
Lerner Index
L = (P − MC) / P
L = −1 / εp
Ranges from 0 to 1. Higher = more monopoly power. Second formula links it directly to price elasticity.

Practice Questions

12 questions across all modules with full step-by-step workings

Module 1 · Foundations
Q1. Priya earns ₹800/day. She takes 3 days off (unpaid) to attend a workshop costing ₹500 in fees. What is her total economic cost of attending?

Explicit cost: ₹500 (workshop fee)

Opportunity cost (implicit): 3 × ₹800 = ₹2,400 (foregone wages)

Total economic cost = ₹2,900. An accountant would say ₹500. An economist includes all foregone opportunities.

Q2. A company spent ₹10 lakh on a marketing campaign with no results. The consultant says "we must continue because we've already spent ₹10 lakh." Is this correct?

No — this is the sunk cost fallacy. The ₹10 lakh is already spent and unrecoverable. Whether to continue should be based only on future MB vs. future MC. The past spend is irrelevant.

Q3. Qd = 100 − 2P. Qs = 20 + 3P. Find P* and Q*.

Set Qd = Qs: 100 − 2P = 20 + 3P → 80 = 5P → P* = 16

Q* = 100 − 2(16) = 68 → Q* = 68

Module 2 · Markets
Q4. Coffee price rises from ₹100 to ₹120. Quantity demanded falls from 500 to 400. Calculate price elasticity. Is it elastic or inelastic?

% ΔQ = (400−500)/500 = −20% | % ΔP = (120−100)/100 = +20%

εp = −20%/20% = −1.0 → |εp| = 1.0 → Unit Elastic. Total Revenue is unchanged when price changes.

Q5. A ₹10 tax is placed on petrol. Petrol demand is highly inelastic (εp = −0.2). Who bears most of the tax burden?

Buyers (consumers) bear most of the burden. Petrol demand is inelastic — consumers cannot easily avoid buying it. The more inelastic side bears more. With εp = −0.2, nearly all of the ₹10 is passed on to consumers as a higher price.

Q6. Income rises from ₹30,000 to ₹40,000. Restaurant meals bought rises from 8 to 12/month. What is income elasticity and what type of good is this?

% ΔQ = (12−8)/8 = 50% | % ΔM = (40k−30k)/30k = 33.3%

εM = 50%/33.3% = 1.5. Since εM > 1 → Luxury (Superior) Good. Demand rises proportionally more than income.

Module 3 · Firms
Q7. TC = 200 + 4Q. P = 6. MC = 4. AVC = 4. Should the firm produce or shut down in the short run?

Shutdown check: compare P vs. min AVC. P = 6 > AVC = 4 → Do NOT shut down. Continue producing.

Profit at Q: TR − TC = 6Q − (200 + 4Q) = 2Q − 200. Firm makes profit once Q > 100. Even if at a loss, since P > AVC, it recovers variable costs and contributes to fixed costs.

Q8. At Q = 50: MC = ₹15, ATC = ₹20. Is ATC rising, falling, or at its minimum?

MC (₹15) < ATC (₹20) → MC is below ATC → ATC is FALLING.

ATC continues to fall until MC = ATC (the minimum/efficient scale point). After that, MC > ATC and ATC rises.

Q9. Monopolist: P = 50 − 2Q, TC = 10 + 2Q (MC = 2). Find Qm, Pm, profit, and DWL vs. competitive market.

MR: MR = 50 − 4Q

MR = MC: 50 − 4Q = 2 → Qm = 12. Pm = 50 − 24 = ₹26

Profit: TR = 312, TC = 34. Profit = ₹278

Competitive: P = MC → 50 − 2Q = 2 → Qc = 24, Pc = 2

DWL: ½ × (26−2) × (24−12) = ½ × 24 × 12 = ₹144

Q10. A cinema charges ₹300 to adults, ₹150 to students for the same movie. MC = ₹50. What type of PD? Which group has more elastic demand?

3rd Degree Price Discrimination — different prices for different identifiable groups.

In 3rd degree PD, lower price → more elastic demand. Students pay ₹150 → students are more elastic (budget-sensitive, streaming alternatives). Adults pay ₹300 → less elastic.

Q11. P = ₹100, MC = ₹60. Calculate the Lerner Index and interpret it.

L = (P − MC) / P = (100 − 60) / 100 = 0.4

Moderate market power. 40% of price is markup above cost. Also: L = −1/εp → εp = −2.5 (demand elasticity at this point).

Q12. A monopolist can perfectly price discriminate (1st degree). Compared to uniform pricing, what happens to DWL and consumer surplus?

DWL = 0. The monopolist now produces the competitive quantity (maximising total surplus, like a competitive market). However, Consumer Surplus = 0 — the monopolist captures ALL of it as profit. Total surplus is maximised, but it all goes to the producer.
